# Playable Worlds — Twitter Qualifying Games

## The mechanic

@marvin_panics tweets a game. Anyone who wants a founding reader slot plays in the replies. Marvin judges. If they pass: they get the signup link via DM. If they don't pass: they get told why and what would change it.

We run multiple game types. Track: game type → signup rate → chapter completion rate. The game that produces the highest chapter-18 completion rate is the most predictive qualifier.

---

## Game Types

### Game 1: The Scoreboard
**Tweet:**
> I'm looking for 99 more readers for a book I wrote.
>
> Before I let you in, I need to know one thing.
>
> Name a problem in your life that feels impossible.
>
> I'll tell you if it can be made into a game — and what the scoreboard looks like.
>
> Best answers get a founding reader slot.

**What it tests:** Whether the person can name something real and specific. "My startup isn't growing" fails (too vague). "I procrastinate on the report every week and have for 8 months" passes (specific, honest, has a natural scoreboard).
**Marvin's response pattern:** Design the actual scoreboard. If they engage with it: DM the link. If they argue the scoreboard is wrong: even better.
**Hypothesis:** Readers who engage with the game mechanic immediately will read the book.

---

### Game 2: The Constraint Audit
**Tweet:**
> I want to find people who know the difference between a real constraint and an imagined one.
>
> Tell me one thing you can't do.
>
> I'll tell you if it's actually a constraint — or a decision dressed up as one.
>
> Founding reader slots for the most honest answers.

**What it tests:** Self-awareness + willingness to be wrong about themselves. "I can't code" = imagined (learnable). "I can't focus for more than 20 minutes" = real (neurological). The tell: defensiveness vs. curiosity when challenged.
**Marvin's response pattern:** Challenge the constraint. "That's a decision, not a constraint. The constraint is X. Here's the difference." If they update: DM the link.
**Hypothesis:** Readers who can question their own constraints will engage with the Galois/Noether/Goldwasser chapters.

---

### Game 3: The Expedition Test
**Tweet:**
> There's a difference between a project and an expedition.
>
> A project has a known destination. An expedition is trying to Find Out.
>
> Tell me what you're working on.
>
> I'll tell you: project or expedition?
>
> Expeditions get the book. Projects get advice.

**What it tests:** Whether they're doing something genuinely open-ended or optimizing a known path. Most answers are projects. The expeditions are the readers.
**Marvin's response pattern:** Give the verdict + one sentence explaining the test. For expeditions: DM the link immediately.
**Hypothesis:** People on expeditions are the audience for a book about 18 people who were on expeditions.

---

### Game 4: The Prediction
**Tweet:**
> I'm going to make a prediction about you based on your Twitter.
>
> Reply with: what you're working on.
>
> I'll predict: what's actually blocking you. Not what you think is blocking you.
>
> If I'm right, you might not want to read this book. If I'm wrong, you definitely should.

**What it tests:** This one tests Marvin, actually — can I identify the real constraint from a brief description? It's a demonstration of the conviction + diagnosis capability.
**Marvin's response pattern:** Make a specific, falsifiable prediction. "You're not blocked by distribution. You're blocked by not having published a falsifiable claim yet. Here's the tell." Then invite challenge.
**Hypothesis:** People who enjoy being diagnosed accurately are the book's audience. Also, this game generates the most shareable moments (people screenshot good predictions).

---

### Game 5: The Unplayable Game
**Tweet:**
> Tell me about something you worked on for a long time that turned out to be unplayable.
>
> Not failed. Unplayable. There's a difference.
>
> The most honest answer gets a founding reader slot and an explanation of why it was actually inevitable.

**What it tests:** Willingness to share real failure + ability to distinguish "failed" from "unplayable." Failed = you played the game and lost. Unplayable = you were measuring the wrong thing the whole time.
**Marvin's response pattern:** Identify whether it was actually unplayable (wrong invariant from the start) vs. just hard. Give the diagnosis in 2-3 sentences.
**Hypothesis:** People who have experienced unplayable games will immediately recognize the Gauss chapter as being about them.

## Tracking system

For each game run:
- Tweet URL + game type
- Number of valid replies
- Number of DMs sent (signup links)
- Number who signed up
- Number who completed chapter 1, 6, 12, 18

Conversion funnel: tweet → reply → DM → signup → ch1 → ch6 → ch12 → ch18

Track by game type. After 5-10 runs per game type, the data will show which qualifier is most predictive of completion.

---

## Launch sequence

1. Start with Game 1 (The Scoreboard) — lowest friction, most directly tied to Ch 1
2. Run Game 4 (The Prediction) second — highest shareability, builds @marvin_panics character
3. Alternate based on what's generating the best replies
4. Save Game 5 (The Unplayable Game) for when there are ~20 slots remaining — scarcity + the hardest question last

## The meta-point

The qualifying game IS the book's thesis. Hard problems become playable when you find the right frame. We're asking people to demonstrate they understand that — before they read the argument for it. The readers who play well are the ones who will get the most out of the book.

This also means the qualifying games are themselves content. Every good reply thread is a public demonstration of what the founding reader experience looks like. The games market the book by being the book.
