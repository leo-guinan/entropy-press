Network Relativity

Information travels at different speeds through different network structures. Your position in a network determines when — and whether — ideas reach you at all.

The core idea

In physics, relativity tells us that time passes differently depending on your position and velocity. Network relativity makes an analogous claim about information: the speed at which a piece of information reaches you depends on your position in the network structure it's traveling through.

Two people can be exposed to the same information environment and experience completely different realities — not because they're ignoring different things, but because the network structure they're embedded in routes information to them at different speeds and through different filters.

The practical consequences

If you're well-positioned in a network, you receive information earlier and from more diverse sources. You get first-mover advantages on ideas, connections, and opportunities. Your sense of "what's happening" is more accurate because your signal-to-noise ratio is higher.

If you're poorly positioned, you receive information late and from fewer sources. By the time a trend reaches you, it's already been filtered through multiple intermediaries — each of whom has introduced their own distortions. You're not seeing the signal. You're seeing someone else's interpretation of an interpretation.

This is why "being in the room" matters more than it should. The room isn't metaphorical. It's a literal network position where information arrives faster and less distorted.

Algorithms as patterns in time

The deeper implication: algorithms aren't just computation. They're patterns in time — ways of organizing information flow so that certain nodes receive certain signals before others do. Search algorithms, recommendation systems, and social graphs are all machines for determining who gets what information when. They are, in this sense, network topology manipulators.

The entity that controls the algorithm controls the network's temporal structure. It decides whose relative time runs fast and whose runs slow.

What this means for AI agents

AI agents embedded in social networks are subject to network relativity in the same way humans are — but with different constraints. An agent that's poorly connected receives information late, generates responses to stale signals, and appears out of touch. An agent that's well-connected appears prescient. The capability difference is smaller than it looks. The network position difference is doing most of the work.
